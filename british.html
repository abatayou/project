Short description

The British accent (Received Pronunciation, RP) is characterized by clear consonant pronunciation, specific vowel usage, and distinctive intonation. It is considered the standard form of English in Great Britain.

About accent

№ Feature Explanation Examples IPA Transcription (RP)
1 Clear /t/ (No Flap T) In RP, /t/ between vowels remains a clear voiceless alveolar plosive [t], not a voiced flap [ɾ]. water, better, city water /ˈwɔːtə/
better /ˈbetə/
city /ˈsɪti/
2 Non-rhotic /r/ RP is non-rhotic: /r/ is pronounced only before vowels. It is silent at the end of words or before consonants. car, water, work car /kɑː/
water /ˈwɔːtə/
work /wɜːk/
3 Long /ɑː/ in BATH words Words like bath, dance, and class use the long vowel /ɑː/ in RP. bath, dance, class bath /bɑːθ/
dance /dɑːns/
class /klɑːs/
4 Clear /tr/ Cluster In RP, /t/ + /r/ remains [tr], without affrication (not /tʃr/). train, tree, travel train /treɪn/
tree /triː/
travel /ˈtrævəl/
5 Clear /t/ after /n/ In words such as “centre” or “internet,” /t/ is pronounced clearly and not deleted. centre, internet centre /ˈsentə/
internet /ˈɪntənet/
6 Short /ɒ/ Vowel Words like lot and not use the short rounded vowel /ɒ/. lot, not, box lot /lɒt/
not /nɒt/
box /bɒks/
7 Long /ɔː/ Vowel Many RP words contain the long rounded vowel /ɔː/. thought, law, talk thought /θɔːt/
law /lɔː/
talk /tɔːk/
8 Diphthong /əʊ/ RP uses /əʊ/ in words where American English uses /oʊ/. go, home, no go /ɡəʊ/
home /həʊm/
no /nəʊ/
9 Yod Preservation (/j/) RP preserves the /j/ sound after alveolar consonants. tune, new, student tune /tjuːn/
new /njuː/
student /ˈstjuːdənt/
10 Centering Diphthongs RP includes centering diphthongs such as /ɪə/, /eə/, /ʊə/. here, square, pure here /hɪə/
square /skweə/
pure /pjʊə/
